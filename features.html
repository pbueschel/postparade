What the incumbents do, what none of them do,

The two-sided matching layer between trainer and track remains unowned. That's the wedge.

The problem, in trainers' own words

Voice-of-customer pass across TDN, BloodHorse, Paulick Report, The Racing Biz, and Past The Wire.

"Racing offices are guilty of not wanting to run races unless the field size is large enough… it is now quite common to card multiple extra races."

Barry Irwin, Team Valor CEO — TDN. You can't plan a campaign against a book that keeps changing.

"Some of us — and I'm one of them — have succumbed to the temptation of playing with the race conditions to get that extra horse or two."

Sal Sinatra, Parx racing secretary — TDN. Even the office admits conditions defeat eligibility math.

Racing office staff "will often tell trainers about the horses in a race to try and make it fill."

David Donk, trainer — Paulick Report (Rice hearings). Pre-draw intel flows by phone, selectively.

Paperwork under HISA doubled, "diverting time from actual horse work."

Jason Barkley, trainer (~30 horses) — TDN/HBPA. Any new platform must reduce total admin, not add a portal.

Trainer-side recommendations

Priority order. In the demo links open the live prototype.

P0 Per-horse eligibility engine — the wedge

In the demo →

Every incumbent gives the trainer a filter; PostParade gives an answer. For each horse, every open race shows a computed YES/NO with the reason — non-winners ladders (N1X/N2L/N$Y), state-bred restrictions, vet's-list holds with the eligible date, Lasix/stakes rules — replacing the error-prone math even racing secretaries admit defeats people.

Beats: InCompass IRO (shows inputs, never computes the answer), Race Trackr/TLore/equineline (filter search). Free "why eligible / why not" is the cheapest trainer-acquisition wedge in the market.

P0 Draw-in probability

In the demo →

The trainer's real anxiety isn't "is there a race" — it's "will I draw in." PostParade models the preference system (date/stars), the field cap, and the also-eligible band into a Likely in / Likely AE / Unlikely call on every recommendation, updating as entries land.

Beats: everything, everywhere — confirmed absent in the US, UK (closing-day counts only), and Australia (Melbourne Cup ballot only). Even Instawork/OpenTable never expose fill probability to users.

P0 Spot alerts — condition-centric standing watches

In the demo →

Inverts Equibase Virtual Stable: instead of "your horse worked," the alert is "a race your horse fits was just published — entries close Thursday." One standing watch per unplaced horse; new books, extras, and substitute races fire push/SMS the moment they match, with a Submit button in the notification.

Beats: Virtual Stable (entity-centric, email digest, hours of latency), Backstretch (post-draw only). Borrowed mechanics: Resy Notify standing alerts + DAT saved-search alarms.

P0 True Purse — expected value per start

In the demo →

One number per recommendation: expected purse earnings + Ship & Win check + state-bred supplement − vanning cost, weighted by draw-in probability. A trainer weighing Del Mar's $5k+50% against a KTDF-flagged Ellis race currently assembles that math from five websites and a phone call.

Beats: nothing surfaces incentives at decision time — rules live in prose, PDFs, and literal JPEGs. DAT RateView proved lane-level money transparency is what supply pays for.

P0 The cut line — preference & also-eligible transparency

In the demo →

For an oversubscribed race, the full field ranked by the track's preference system with the draw-in cut and AE band marked — your barn highlighted. This is the intel the racing office shares selectively by phone today (see the Rice hearings), democratized.

Beats: the UK's elimination-sequence panel (closing-day only) — we show it from the moment entries open.

P1 Deadline command center

Partially in demo

Per-horse countdown chips for every deadline that costs money when missed: entry close, scratch time, stakes nomination windows, and supplement registrations (an unregistered KY-bred forfeits a ~$27k average KTDF supplement). Modeled on Racing Australia's daily state-by-state deadline summary.

In demo: entry-close countdowns + KTDF registration rail on Spot alerts. Next: nomination windows, scratch clocks, HIWU clearance SLA countdowns.

P1 Vet's-list clearance clock

Gate in demo

List status is already a hard eligibility gate in the demo (barred-until-date, recently-cleared badges). The full feature adds the clearance path: required workout/bloodwork steps, the HIWU 5-business-day SLA countdown, and a computed "earliest safe entry date" — preventing the documented race-day-scratch failure mode.

P1 Extra / overnight lifecycle tracking

Planned

When a race fails to fill, auto-follow it: "brought back as an extra for Thursday — needs 3 more, your horse fits." Turns West Point's documented weeks-long drawing-board loop into a push alert, and closes the loop with the track-side extras board below.

P2 Owner guest view

Parked by design

A read-only, per-horse "where we plan to run and why" card — recommendation, eligibility, draw-in odds, incentive — that the trainer shares for owner approval, with auto-posted milestones ("Drew in, post 6"). TLore OwnerSync, TRM, and Prism prove owner comms drive trainer stickiness. The owner workspace screens already exist in the prototype, parked for exactly this.

Track-side recommendations

The racing office has no incumbent to displace — this side of the market is a whiteboard and a phone.

P0 Race Fill Board — fill probability per race

In the demo →

The racing office "board," digitized: every carded race with live entries vs the minimum, a will-it-go probability, and hours-to-close — plus cancellation early warning on the strength board (10 lack-of-entries cancellations in Sept 2025 alone). This is the secretary's home screen.

Beats: InCompass IRO's Rundown (raw counts, one track, no forecast). Racehorse 360 does adjacent work but is proprietary to 1/ST's own tracks.

P0 Targeted Request outreach with attached incentives

In the demo →

When a race is short, the engine ranks every eligible, fit, geographically-reachable horse — with the trainer's likelihood to accept and the applicable Ship & Win bonus attached — and the office sends one-tap Requests instead of working a phone tree for 24 hours. Requests convert to entries when the trainer accepts.

Beats: the phone. Literally nothing else exists. Borrowed mechanics: Instawork tiered dispatch, Wonolo preferred lists, DAT LaneMakers reverse prospecting ("trainers who have shipped this lane before").

P1 Incentive publisher + ROI dashboard

Modeled in demo

A structured form where the office defines starter checks, purse-bonus percentages, and eligibility predicates — which then render on every matching horse's recommendation and power targeted Requests. Plus the analytics nobody sells: cost per added starter and projected handle lift per bonus dollar, so mid-tier tracks can size a program the way Del Mar sizes Ship & Win. Demo already models Ship & Win as executable eligibility rules with a budget cap.

P2 Condition demand testing

Planned

Before the book is published, float draft conditions to the trainer base and see indicated interest (soft commits by eligible horses). Attacks the 8-hours-to-card-8-races problem from the demand side — and the aggregated trainer-intent data is exactly what every AI-condition-book effort (Pinnacle AI, Racehorse 360) lacks.

P2 Multi-track stall applications

Planned

One stable profile submitted to any participating meet, with in-app status instead of confirmation by mail, start-per-stall quota tracking, and the trainer's intended race schedule attached — giving the secretary a horses-per-condition forecast at stall-allocation time.

Roadmap · not current priority

Jockeys & agents — a validated future lane

Per direction, jockeys are not the current priority — but the research confirms the lane is real and empty: no US jockey-booking software exists at all. The UK books riders inside the declaration transaction; NZ/AU startups (JockeyFinder, JockeyHQ) prove agents want offer state machines and availability calendars. Our path when the time comes: (1) an optional "intended rider" field on every Submit — the natural seam; (2) an agent workspace reusing the existing Submit/Request negotiation plumbing (requested → accepted → confirmed, with an audit trail); (3) a "rider secured" badge on ship-in Requests, since an unbooked rider is a scratch risk that hurts field size. A live per-meet jockey/agent directory replaces the static PDF lists tracks publish today and gets agents into the product cheaply.

Strategic notes from the research

  • Monetize the track side; keep trainers cheap. Trainer willingness-to-pay is thin ($10–30/mo tools survive; $125/mo Race Trackr didn't). Tracks already spend millions on untargeted incentives. Free alerts are the acquisition wedge; per-meet SaaS + pay-on-start incentive placement (OpenTable's $7.50-per-seated-cover model) is the revenue.
  • Sign circuits, not tracks. USTA harness proved breed-wide entry rails work when a central body standardizes; per-track sales is why IRO adoption stalled. One anchor meet activates its circuit's trainers.
  • Enter-once, reuse everywhere. HISA doubled paperwork; the strongest antidote to yet-another-portal resistance is one stable profile that pre-fills every Submit (IRO's exact field set: conditions, meds, equipment, rider, weight).
  • Be the software layer, never the principal. Convoy automated 98% of matching and still died taking transaction risk; DAT paid ~$250M for the software layer. PostParade sits beside Equibase and InCompass — the official entry can still land in RTO.
  • Identity by registration number. Virtual Stable's documented wrong-horse-by-name failures → key horses to registration/tattoo/microchip from day one.
  • Integrate barn management, don't rebuild it. Stable Secretary/BarnManager own barn ops with zero racing features; HorseLinc shows a thin tool without a data moat stalls. Our moat is the eligibility/draw-in engine on Equibase-shaped data.
